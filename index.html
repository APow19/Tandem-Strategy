Credentials

What is a CGMA — and why does it matter?

The Chartered Global Management Accountant designation is awarded by CIMA and AICPA, the world's two largest accounting bodies. It represents the gold standard in management accounting — going beyond bookkeeping to strategic financial leadership.

CGMA holders are trained to interpret financial data in the context of real business decisions: pricing, investment, cost structure, and risk. It's the difference between a finance function that reports what happened and one that shapes what happens next.

For SMEs, engaging a CGMA-qualified consultant means getting the kind of financially-led strategic thinking that was previously only available to large corporates — without the overhead of a full-time FD.

Cash Flow

The working capital cycle — and why it matters more than profit

A business can be profitable on paper and still run out of cash. The working capital cycle explains why: it measures the gap between when you pay your suppliers and when your customers pay you. If that gap is wide, your money is tied up in the business rather than in your bank account.

Two numbers define this cycle. DSO (Days Sales Outstanding) is how long customers take to pay you — calculated by dividing what they owe you by your credit sales, multiplied by the days in your period. DPO (Days Payable Outstanding) is how long you take to pay suppliers, using the same method with your supplier balances and purchases.

Subtract DPO from DSO and you get your cash gap. A positive number means you are funding your customers. A negative number means your suppliers are effectively funding you — a strong commercial position. Reducing DSO by even 5–10 days, or extending supplier terms by the same, can unlock significant working capital without a single extra sale.

Margins

Markup vs Margin — a confusion that erodes profit

These two terms are frequently confused, but the difference is significant. Markup is the amount added to cost to arrive at a selling price, expressed as a percentage of cost. Margin is the profit expressed as a percentage of the selling price.

A product that costs £80 and sells for £100 has a 25% markup but only a 20% margin. If you're pricing for a "20% margin" using markup calculations, you're systematically undercharging — a compounding problem as revenue scales.

Getting this right across your entire product range, including all landed costs in international purchasing, is one of the quickest ways to recover hidden profitability.

Legislation

The Late Payment Act — your rights, plainly explained

The Late Payment of Commercial Debts (Interest) Act 1998 gives businesses the statutory right to charge interest on overdue invoices at 8% above the Bank of England base rate — automatically, without it needing to be stated in your terms.

You can also claim a fixed compensation amount: £40 for debts under £1,000, £70 for £1,000–£9,999, and £100 for £10,000 or more. These rights exist to protect smaller suppliers from being used as involuntary lenders by larger customers.

In practice, simply referencing the Act in your payment reminder letters — particularly the statutory interest figure — is often enough to accelerate payment without the relationship cost of formal action.
